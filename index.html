The Scientific Revolution

A fundamental transformation in how humans understood the natural world — from religious authority to observation and reason.

Causation Focus: What caused the Scientific Revolution? Was it Renaissance humanism, cross-cultural exchange (Ibn al-Shatir to Copernicus), the Reformation's challenge to authority, or new instruments?
SOURCES REVIEWED0 / 4
Primary Source · Scientific Revolution
Nicolaus Copernicus — On the Revolution of Heavenly Bodies
1543▾
Visual diagram: Copernicus' heliocentric model places Sol (the Sun) at the center, orbited by Mercury, Venus, Earth (with the Moon), Mars, Jupiter, and Saturn — a direct challenge to centuries of Church-endorsed geocentric cosmology.

Those who know that the consensus of many centuries has sanctioned the conception that the earth remains at rest in the middle of the heaven... would regard it as an insane pronouncement if I made the opposite assertion that the earth moves. Therefore I debated with myself for a long time whether to publish...

By long and intense study I finally found that if the motions of the other planets are correlated with the orbiting of the earth, not only do their phenomena follow therefrom but also the order and size of all the planets and spheres, and heaven itself is so linked together that in no portion of it can anything be shifted without disrupting the remaining parts and the universe as a whole.

Comprehension Questions

1. Why did Copernicus hesitate to publish? What does this tell us about the risks of challenging accepted authority?

2. What does placing the Sun at the center change about how people understand humanity's place in the universe?

Visual Source · Scientific Revolution
Ibn al-Shatir — Lunar Model
14th Century▾
Ibn al-Shatir was a Muslim astronomer in 14th-century Damascus. His planetary models — developed roughly 150 years before Copernicus — used mathematical techniques nearly identical to those Copernicus later used. His lunar model shows the distance of the Moon from Earth using observational geometric proofs.
Comprehension Questions

1. Ibn al-Shatir's work predates and may have influenced Copernicus. How does this complicate the idea that the Scientific Revolution was purely a European achievement?

2. What does the similarity between Ibn al-Shatir's and Copernicus' mathematical methods suggest about how scientific knowledge traveled?

As we were at one time children... numerous prejudices stand in the way of our arriving at the knowledge of truth; and of these it seems impossible for us to rid ourselves, unless we undertake, once in our lifetime, to doubt of all those things in which we may discover even the smallest suspicion of uncertainty.

II. That we ought also to consider as false all that is doubtful.

Comprehension Questions

Philosophers applied reason to human society — government, rights, economics, and religion. These ideas sparked revolutions around the world.

1784▾

Enlightenment is man's emergence from his self-incurred immaturity. Immaturity is the inability to use one's own understanding without the guidance of another... The motto of enlightenment is therefore: Sapere aude! Dare to understand! Have courage to use your own understanding!

Laziness and cowardice are the reasons why such a large proportion of men... gladly remain immature for life... It is so convenient to be immature!

The Causation Thesis Formula

Although [acknowledge other causes/factors], the most significant
[cause OR effect] of [your topic] was
[your argument] because [reason 1] and [reason 2].
Model Example
"Although the printing press and political tensions with Rome also contributed, the most significant cause of the Protestant Reformation was widespread corruption in the Catholic Church because the sale of indulgences directly contradicted scripture and destroyed ordinary Christians' trust in Church leadership."
